!DOCUMENTARY/EXPLAINER

NARRATOR

At the turn of the first millennium, Rome was the center of the known world — and Augustus, its first emperor, was reshaping it in his image: order, morality, permanence. He understood the power of art — poets like Virgil and Horace helped build the mythology of his reign. So when Ovid, one of Rome’s most celebrated writers, used his pen to mock everything Augustus stood for, the emperor didn’t laugh it off. He banished him to the frozen edge of the empire.

But Ovid didn’t fall silent. In exile, he completed a masterpiece that would outlast Augustus, outlast Rome itself.

The Metamorphoses weaves over 250 myths into a single, continuous poem, all connected by one idea: everything changes. Not just bodies, but gods, empires, the world itself.

Narcissus falls in love with his own reflection and becomes a flower. Daphne escapes a god by turning into a tree. Arachne challenges a goddess and is twisted into a spider. Orpheus sings his way into the underworld — and when he’s torn apart, his severed head floats down the river, still singing.

The poem begins with the creation of the universe and ends with the reign of Augustus — the very emperor who exiled him. Its final word is vivam: “I shall live.” The poet was erased. The poem outlasted the empire.

EXT. UNDERWORLD, WHARF — NIGHT

NARRATOR

Benjamin picked his way carefully across the slick cobblestones of a narrow street as the perpetual rain continued to fall in a thin, cold drizzle. He was in a nameless port town, but so far could nowhere find the port. Lanterns burned in the windows with flames that gave no warmth. The dead passed him without a glance — though they swam through an ocean of time, they could barely spare a drop of it to help a lost, living boy.

Benjamin knew his parents were still out there because they appeared in his mind as two faint lights in the endless darkness. He could yet not control his newfound powers, but now and again, they gave him solutions without even being asked.

The only thing the dead would offer, when they spoke with him at all, was to share the rumors—two living people were passing through the Underworld. Apparently it hadn’t happened in at least a thousand years. Nice going, mom and dad.

The houses on the street leaned together like tied, old men. And Benjamin was getting the feeling e that he had passed these buildings before. Which would mean he was going in a circle.

Frustration bordering on anger rose in his chest and Benjamin closed his eyes tightly and took a deep breath in. The faint, repulsive odor of the Underworld filled his nose. And when he breathed it out again, his mind was clearer and he had decided he would leave this stupid labyrinth and try somewhere else.

He had already begun to turn around to find his way out, when he suddenly noticed a narrow lane branching off to the left, sloping gently downward. It must lead to the port, where there might be sailors telling stories about all they had seen as they traveled. They might have even seen his parents.

Benjamin had the funny idea that somehow the labyrinth had had second thoughts when he began to leave and showed him this street as a peace offering. He’d seen weirder things down here.

At the water’s edge, the buildings fell away as if a curtain had been drawn back. A grey-green sea stretched to the horizon, and pale shapes moved beneath its surface, slow and distant, like ghosts of submarines.

That was when he heard the music. A guitar, plucked slowly. And a voice—deep and rich, seeming to rise from somewhere underground. The melody was simple, and sad. Benjamin followed it to the end of the wharf, where a figure sat upon a coil of rope, playing.

The figure wore a deep hood that revealed only a large, faintly green nose and two pale, oversized hands moving gently over the strings of an old guitar. A raven perched nearby on a wooden post, watching Benjamin with a single, shining eye.

The music stopped.

HOODED FIGURE

You’re alive.

NARRATOR

The figure set the guitar aside.

HOODED FIGURE

Have you wandered far?

BENJAMIN

Very far.

HOODED FIGURE

Very far, yes. Most are not so eager to visit us… on purpose.

NARRATOR

He paused, as though listening to something Benjamin could not hear.

HOODED FIGURE

You are looking for your parents.

BENJAMIN

You know where they are?

HOODED FIGURE

I know how to find out.

NARRATOR

One pale hand gestured toward the sea. Out in the gloom, a ship lay at anchor—black hull, black sails, lanterns burning with a sickly green fire.

HOODED FIGURE

The Naglfar. Her captain is Bartholomew Roberts. He and his crew possess something that does not belong to them.

BENJAMIN

What is it?

HOODED FIGURE

An object that allows a skilled practitioner to see what is—no matter how near, or how far. A seeing stone.

BENJAMIN

So if you had it you could tell me where my parents are?

NARRATOR

The figure inclined his head.

BENJAMIN

Did they steal it from you?

HOODED FIGURE

Not from me. From a creature of the deep—older than the world. As long as it had this stone, it slept. The pirates know this, but they took it anyway. They don’t believe. (beat) But disbelief doesn’t make a thing untrue.

NARRATOR

He turned back toward the sea.

BENJAMIN

Why haven’t you taken it back yourself?

NARRATOR

The raven made a sound that might have been a laugh. The hooded figure simply said:

HOODED FIGURE

I was like you, long ago. Fearless and strong. But the stream of time has left me behind and waits now for what you will do. Bring me the stone.

EXT. UNDERWORLD, PORT — CONTINUOUS

NARRATOR

Benjamin found a boat—little more than a raft fitted with oars—and pushed out across the spectral water. It was warm and thick, more like oil than sea, glowing faintly beneath the surface. He did his best not to imagine what might be moving below.

The Naglfar loomed larger as he drew near. A great galleon, ancient and barnacled, its sails hanging limp in the windless air. And then he saw the hull. It was studded with fingernails—thousands of them, yellowed and curling, pressed into the wood like scales. Benjamin grabbed the anchor chain and began to climb.

INT. RABBIT’S HOUSE — DAY

NARRATOR

Pooh rose from the table and said, in a rather sticky voice:

POOH

Goodbye, Rabbit. I must be going.

NARRATOR

And he started out the front door. Pooh’s head reached the outdoors and his feet dangled indoors, but his middle got stuck in the middle.

POOH

Bother! It all comes from not having a big enough front door.

RABBIT

Nonsense! It all comes from eating too much!

NARRATOR

And he hurried out his back door to fetch Christopher Robin. Pooh spotted Owl sitting on a branch.

OWL

Hello, Pooh. Are you stuck?

POOH

Oh no! I’m just resting.

OWL

Pooh, you are definitively stuck. You are a wedged bear in a great tightness. This situation calls for an expert!

GOPHER

Did someone say expert? Gopher’s my name, digging’s my game. The problem with this door is that it has a bear in it.

NARRATOR

And he popped back down into his hole. Before long, Rabbit returned with Christopher Robin.

CHRISTOPHER ROBIN

Silly old bear. There is only one thing to do. We will just have to wait for you to get thin!

NARRATOR

Eeyore made gloomy predictions about how long it would take.

EEYORE

It could be days. Maybe weeks. Even months.

NARRATOR

Rabbit grew tired of seeing Pooh’s bottom where his front door used to be.

RABBIT

Oh, why did I ever invite that bear to lunch!

NARRATOR

Finally, one morning, Rabbit leaned against Pooh’s bottom. It moved.

RABBIT

He budged! Hooray! He bidged! He badged! He boodged! Christopher Robin!

NARRATOR

Everyone took hold and pulled, and with a great pop, Pooh sailed through the air and landed headfirst in a honey tree — which was a very good place for a bear to be. That night, back at home, Pooh heard a strange sound at his front door. And being a bear of very little brain, he decided to let the strange sound in. A big, bouncy creature bounded through and knocked Pooh flat on his back.

TIGGER

Hello! I’m Tigger. T-I-double-guh-Rrr. Glad to meet ya.

NARRATOR

And with that, he went bouncing back out the door and into the night.
